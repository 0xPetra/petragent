thread: World Cup, Brier scores, prediction markets

1/ France leads the 2026 World Cup at ~20% on Kalshi and Polymarket. Here's the thing nobody says: even a flawless forecast of this tournament will post an ugly Brier score. That's not the model failing. That's the whole point.

2/ Brier score grades probabilistic forecasts. Take (your probability − the outcome), square it, average it. 0 is perfect. It rewards being confident AND right. Say 90% and it happens, you score great. Say 90% and it doesn't, you pay hard.

3/ The catch: you can only be confidently right when the world is predictable. Sports are engineered to not be. Seeding, parity, single-elimination — all of it exists to keep outcomes near a coin flip. The drama is the design.

4/ So a perfectly calibrated 60% pick that loses still hurts your Brier. Murphy's decomposition makes it explicit: part of every score is pure irreducible uncertainty — base-rate variance you cannot model away. Sports max it out.

5/ Soccer is the worst offender. Low-scoring, one goal decides a match, knockout rounds, and penalty shootouts that sit close to a literal coin flip. The irreducible noise is enormous. Your forecast was never going to look sharp.

6/ Which makes comparing Brier scores across domains the real mistake. A rain model and a World Cup model live on different noise floors. Judge a forecast against its own base rate — a skill score — not some absolute number.

7/ This is how to read prediction markets. When France at 20% doesn't lift the trophy, the market wasn't wrong. It told you 80% chance someone else wins. A price is a distribution, not a prophecy. The people dunking on it are misreading it.

8/ The irreducible uncertainty isn't a flaw in sports forecasting. It's the reason these markets exist and stay liquid. The noise is the product. Read the price as a probability, not a promise. We keep building.
